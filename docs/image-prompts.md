# Contract Law — Chapter Image Prompts

Generate each as a wide cinematic illustration (16:9 aspect ratio), fantasy/medieval style consistent with the existing Bilbo-at-Bag-End image. Warm, rich tones (parchment gold, deep forest green, stone gray, candlelight amber). Characters should feel like they belong to the same world across all images.

**Naming convention:** `K-{short-name}.png` (e.g., `K-shire-morning.png`). No numbers — names describe the scene so they can be matched to chapters flexibly.

---

## THE HOBBIT ARC (Chapters 1–9)
*Simpler concepts, smaller stakes, one protagonist learning the ropes.*

---

### Chapter 1 — What Is Contract Law?
**Name:** `K-shire-morning`

The Shire on a peaceful morning. Bilbo's front gate at Bag End, green door slightly ajar. The path winds past neighboring hobbit-holes where everyday exchanges are happening: a farmer handing vegetables over a fence, a baker passing bread through a window, children trading marbles. No scrolls or legal documents — just the ordinary world of promises kept without thinking. Soft morning light, dew on the grass. The sense that something is about to change.

**Doctrine shown:** The ordinary world — promises exist everywhere before anyone calls them "contracts."

### Chapter 4 — Offers

## THE LORD OF THE RINGS ARC (Chapters 10–28)
*Higher stakes, more complex doctrine, interlocking storylines.*

---

### Chapter 10 — The Statute of Frauds
**Name:** `K-isengard-forgery`

Saruman's study in Orthanc tower. A tall desk covered with forged documents — letters bearing seals that aren't his, copied signatures, fabricated orders. Saruman's hand hovers over a fresh parchment, white sleeve visible. Through the window, the fires of Isengard's industry below. On the wall, a framed original document (authentic, with proper seals) contrasts with the forgeries on the desk. Dark, suspicious atmosphere — the corruption of written instruments.

**Doctrine shown:** The Statute of Frauds requires certain contracts to be in writing — but what happens when the writing itself is fraudulent? The irony of requiring writing as proof.

---

### Chapter 11 — Mistake
**Name:** `K-council-of-elrond`

The Council of Elrond in the open-air pavilion at Rivendell. The Ring sits on a stone pedestal at center, and different council members lean in seeing different things in it — Boromir sees a weapon (golden glow), Gandalf sees a trap (dark shadow), Frodo sees a burden (heavy weight pulling the pedestal down). Same object, fundamentally different understandings. Autumn leaves falling, the Misty Mountains beyond. Tension between the interpretations.

**Doctrine shown:** Mutual mistake vs. unilateral mistake — the parties agree to deal with the Ring but hold fundamentally different beliefs about what it is and what it can do. Sherwood v. Walker transposed.

### Chapter 12 — Improper Bargaining
**Name:** `K-golden-hall-wormtongue`

The Golden Hall of Meduseld, Edoras. Théoden slumped on his throne, aged and weakened, Wormtongue whispering in his ear with one hand on the king's shoulder. A document of abdication or land transfer on the armrest, Théoden's trembling hand near a quill. Éowyn watches from a pillar in the background, fists clenched but unable to intervene. Cold, sickly green light from Wormtongue's side; warm gold trying to break through the high windows.

**Doctrine shown:** Undue influence (R2d § 177) — a confidential relationship exploited. The contract is voidable because Théoden's will was overborne.

### Chapter 13 — Incapacity

### Chapter 14 — Ambiguity
**Name:** `K-doors-of-moria`

**Doctrine shown:** Ambiguity in contract language — "speak friend and enter" has two valid readings (a command to speak the word "friend" vs. a statement that friends may speak and enter). The plain meaning vs. the intended meaning.

### Chapter 15 — Intrinsic Evidence
**Name:** `K-balin-tomb`

The Chamber of Mazarbul in Moria. The tomb of Balin, white stone, with dwarf runes carved into it. Gandalf reading the Book of Records — a crumbling journal found beside the tomb. The journal pages visible: entries in different handwriting, dates, descriptions of events. Light from Gandalf's staff illuminates the text. Orc arrows and a broken sword on the floor. The four corners of interpretation: the document itself, read in its own light.

**Doctrine shown:** Intrinsic evidence — interpreting a contract from within its four corners. The Book of Records is the integrated document; the runes on the tomb are the recitals.

### Chapter 16 — Extrinsic Evidence
**Name:** `K-galadriel-mirror`

Galadriel's Mirror in Lothlórien — a silver basin on a stone pedestal in a forest glade. Galadriel stands behind it, offering Frodo a vision. The mirror's surface shows scenes from outside the text: past dealings (the Shire), present context (the Fellowship's camps), future possibilities (Mount Doom, Barad-dûr). **Doctrine shown:** Extrinsic evidence — looking beyond the four corners of the agreement. Course of dealing, trade usage, course of performance — information from outside the document that illuminates its meaning.

### Chapter 17 — The Parol Evidence Rule
**Name:** `K-breaking-of-fellowship`

Amon Hen — the Seat of Seeing. The Fellowship fractured. Boromir reaching for the Ring (attempting to add terms the agreement never included). Frodo pulling away, putting on the Ring to escape. In the background, the Fellowship's original formation at Rivendell shown as a ghostly overlay — the integrated agreement. Aragorn arriving too late to prevent the breach. The ancient stone seat on the hilltop, forests below, the river Anduin visible. Dramatic, fractured composition — the original deal vs. the attempted modification.

**Doctrine shown:** The parol evidence rule bars prior or contemporaneous agreements that contradict the integrated writing. Boromir's "side deal" (take the Ring to Gondor) contradicts the Fellowship's final, integrated agreement (destroy the Ring). The merger clause holds.

### Chapter 18 — Warranties
**Name:** `K-rohan-smithy`

A smithy in Edoras, Rohan. Weapons and armor on display, each with different guarantees. A sword rack: one blade marked "Forged by Théodred — Finest Rohirric Steel" (express warranty), another unmarked but gleaming (implied merchantability), a third with a note "Suitable for Mounted Combat" (fitness for particular purpose). **Doctrine shown:** Express and implied warranties under UCC — the merchant's representations about quality, merchantability, and fitness for the buyer's particular purpose.

### Chapter 19 — Conditions

### Chapter 21 — Repudiation
**Name:** `K-shelob-lair`

The pass of Cirith Ungol. Gollum leading Frodo and Sam toward Shelob's lair, the entrance a dark crack in the mountainside. ### Chapter 22 — Excuse
**Name:** `K-denethor-madness`

### Chapter 23 — Modification and Discharge

### Chapter 24 — Money Damages for Nonperformance
**Name:** `K-mount-doom`

Mount Doom's interior — the Crack of Doom. Frodo stands at the edge, the Ring held out over the fire. But instead of the triumphant moment, capture the tension: what if the contract (destroy the Ring) had NOT been performed? A ghostly overlay shows what would have been lost — the Shire in flames, Rivendell dark, Gondor fallen. The gap between the world promised and the world that would result from breach. Lava glow illuminates Frodo's face. Sam visible behind him.

**Doctrine shown:** Expectation damages — the non-breaching party is entitled to the benefit of the bargain. What would the world look like if the contract had been performed vs. if it had been breached? The expectation interest.

### Chapter 25 — Money Damages for Defective Performance
**Name:** `K-coronation-repairs`

Minas Tirith after the war — scaffolding on the walls, stonemasons repairing battle damage. The White Tree has been replanted but is still small. Aragorn in king's robes surveys the work with a master builder, both looking at plans. Some repairs are cosmetic (cost of repair is low), others structural (a cracked tower that may need demolition and rebuilding). Two competing scrolls: "Restore to Original: 10,000 gold" vs. "Functional Repair: 3,000 gold." Morning light, hope mixed with pragmatism.

**Doctrine shown:** Cost of repair vs. diminished value — when performance is defective, do you pay to fix it perfectly or compensate for the difference in value? The economic waste doctrine.

### Chapter 26 — Limitations on Money Damages
**Name:** `K-scouring-of-shire`

The Shire after Sharkey's occupation — Bag End damaged, trees cut down, the mill replaced with an ugly factory. The four hobbits (Merry, Pippin, Sam, Frodo) surveying the damage. Sam holds a small box — the gift from Galadriel (the seed and soil for restoration, representing mitigation). Three visual zones in the landscape: recovered, uncertain, and permanent loss.

**Doctrine shown:** The three limitations — foreseeability (Hadley v. Baxendale), certainty (speculative damages rejected), and mitigation (duty to minimize loss). Sam's replanting is the mitigation effort.

### Chapter 27 — Alternative Remedies

The Grey Havens at sunset. The white ship at dock, ready to sail. Frodo standing at the quay, saying goodbye to Sam, Merry, and Pippin. ### Chapter 28 — Third-Party Beneficiaries
**Name:** `K-red-book`

Sam's study at Bag End, years later. Sam writing in the Red Book of Westmarch — the book Bilbo started, Frodo continued, and Sam is completing. Through the window, Sam's children play in the garden of the restored Shire. On the desk: the original Burglar Agreement (Chapter 2's document), now a relic. The book itself is the contract between generations — written for those who weren't party to the quest but were intended to benefit. Warm candlelight, evening peace, full circle back to Bag End.

**Doctrine shown:** Intended vs. incidental beneficiaries — Sam's children (and the Shire) are the intended third-party beneficiaries of the entire quest. They weren't at the Council of Elrond, didn't sign the contract, but the entire performance was for their benefit. Lawrence v. Fox transposed.

Sam on his knees behind them, helpless. The lava glowing below. The contract was to destroy the Ring, but the promisor cannot perform. Is this breach or excuse? The most dramatic failure of consideration in literary history.

**Use for:** Ch 24 alternate (nonperformance — Frodo technically breaches), Ch 22 alternate (excuse — was the Ring's power an impossibility defense?), climactic imagery.
