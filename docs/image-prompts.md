# Contract Law Chapter Image Prompts

Generate each as a wide cinematic illustration (16:9 aspect ratio), fantasy/medieval style with subtle legal document elements woven in. Warm, rich tones (parchment gold, deep forest green, stone gray). Characters should be recognizable across images.

## Chapter 1 — Introduction to Contract Law
A weathered scroll unfurled on an oak table in a candlelit study at Bag End. Bilbo Baggins examining the scroll with a magnifying glass, quill and ink nearby. Through the round window, the Shire's green hills. Legal seals and wax stamps visible on the parchment. The scroll reads "On the Nature of Promises" in elegant script.

## Chapter 2 — What Is a Contract?
A medieval marketplace crossroads with four paths marked by stone pillars. Each pillar has a carved word: "Promise," "Agreement," "Bargain," "Exchange." A young hobbit stands at the center deciding which path to take. Merchants trading goods in the background. Warm afternoon light.

## Chapter 3 — Mutual Assent
Two figures — a tall man in riding clothes and a shorter farmer — shaking hands firmly across a wooden fence at twilight. Their handshake is centered and dramatic. Behind each, their respective properties (a fine horse stable, a modest farmstead). A document visible on the fence post between them.

## Chapter 4 — Offers
A town crier standing on the steps of a stone courthouse in a busy medieval village square, reading from an official proclamation. Citizens gathered around, some interested, some passing by. The proclamation scroll is prominently displayed. Market stalls in the background.

## Chapter 5 — Termination of the Offer
A messenger on horseback racing down a forest road toward a distant castle, carrying a sealed letter. Behind the messenger, a second rider is gaining — carrying a different letter (the revocation). An hourglass embedded in the landscape, sand nearly run out. Dramatic sunset.

## Chapter 6 — Acceptance
Two dwarven smiths at a forge, one holding out a finished shield, the other reaching to accept it. Between them, a written order and a modified acknowledgment on the anvil. Sparks from the forge illuminate both documents. The shields on the wall behind them each have slightly different designs (battle of the forms).

## Chapter 7 — Consideration
A merchant's scale (balance) in a jeweler's workshop. On one side, a bag of gold coins. On the other, a single acorn with a small oak seedling growing from it (representing the adequacy question). The jeweler examining both sides thoughtfully. Legal texts open on the workbench.

## Chapter 8 — Promissory Estoppel
An elderly craftsman who has demolished his old workshop and begun building a new one, based on a lord's promise of patronage. The lord's figure is walking away in the distance. Half-built walls, scattered tools, the craftsman looking at a letter (the broken promise). Emotional, sympathetic lighting.

## Chapter 9 — Promissory Restitution
A traveler collapsed on a mountain pass being aided by a stranger — given food, water, shelter. Years later (split scene or visual transition), the now-prosperous traveler's hand signing a document promising payment to the aged rescuer. Gratitude and obligation visible in both scenes.

## Chapter 10 — The Statute of Frauds
A stone tablet being carved by a scribe in a great hall, listing categories of agreements that must be in writing. A line of petitioners waiting, some with written contracts (admitted), some with only oral promises (turned away by a guard). The tablet prominently shows "One Year," "Land," "Goods > 500."

## Chapter 11 — Mistake
Two merchants examining a cow in a barn. One sees a prize breeding animal (painted in golden light), the other sees ordinary livestock (painted in plain gray). The same cow, two different perceptions. A contract of sale between them on a hay bale. Sherwood v. Walker visual metaphor.

## Chapter 12 — Improper Bargaining
A dark, cramped room where a wealthy figure looms over a smaller person at a desk, pressuring them to sign a document. The smaller person's hand trembles. Outside the window, a fair marketplace where honest dealing happens in daylight. Contrast between coercion (inside) and freedom (outside).

## Chapter 13 — Incapacity
A young person (clearly a minor) at a merchant's counter, trying to return a purchased item (a fancy cloak). The merchant gestures at a "NO RETURNS" sign. Between them, a contract with one signature crossed out. Legal books stacked nearby showing "Age of Majority."

## Chapter 14 — Introduction to Interpretation
A ancient stone doorway with runes carved above it. Two scholars stand before it, each reading the same inscription but pointing in different directions (different interpretations). One holds Williston's treatise, the other Corbin's. The runes glow with ambiguity.

## Chapter 15 — Intrinsic Evidence & Canons
A library in a tower, with a judge seated at a high desk reading a contract by candlelight. Around the judge, floating text fragments — clauses, provisos, recitals — arranged in hierarchical order. A magnifying glass reveals hidden meaning in the plain text. Books of canons on shelves.

## Chapter 16 — Extrinsic Evidence
A courtroom scene where witnesses are testifying. One witness holds a stack of prior correspondence. Another demonstrates trade customs by showing samples of goods. A third points to a map showing course of dealing. The judge weighs whether to admit each.

## Chapter 17 — The Parol Evidence Rule
A large ornate door (representing the integrated agreement) with a guard blocking entry. Outside, several people try to enter carrying oral promises, prior drafts, and side letters. Some papers have a green "CONSISTENT" stamp (allowed in), others a red "CONTRADICTS" stamp (barred). The door bears a wax seal reading "FINAL."

## Chapter 18 — Warranties
A merchant's display of swords, each with a tag. One tag reads "Finest Elven Steel" (express warranty). Another has no tag but gleams with quality (implied merchantability). A third has a tag reading "Perfect for Dragon-Slaying" (fitness for particular purpose). A buyer examines each carefully.

## Chapter 19 — Conditions
A drawbridge mechanism with interlocking gears. Each gear is labeled: "If," "Unless," "Provided That," "On Condition." The bridge is half-raised — some conditions met, others not. A knight waits to cross. One gear is broken (condition failed), preventing the bridge from lowering.

## Chapter 20 — Performance and Breach
A master builder presenting a completed great hall to a lord. The hall is magnificent but has one wrong-colored stone in the foundation (Jacob & Youngs). The lord points at the stone with displeasure. The builder gestures at the grandeur of the whole structure. Substantial performance visualized.

## Chapter 21 — Repudiation
A scene at a dock where a ship is being loaded for a promised voyage. The captain announces the ship will not sail (anticipatory repudiation). Merchants who have prepared cargo stand in dismay. One merchant drafts a letter demanding "adequate assurance of performance." Storm clouds gathering.

## Chapter 22 — Excuse
A grand coronation hall prepared for a ceremony — decorated, seats arranged, bunting hung. But the ceremony has been cancelled (frustration of purpose). Workers begin dismantling. Through the window, the reason: the royal procession has been rerouted. The contract for renting the hall lies on an empty chair.

## Chapter 23 — Modification and Discharge
Two craftsmen at a workbench modifying an existing blueprint. The original contract is pinned to the wall. They draw new lines over old ones, adding and removing terms. A fresh piece of parchment (the modification) sits between them. One exchanges new consideration (a bag of coins) while the other nods.

## Chapter 24 — Expectation Damages
A merchant standing in an empty warehouse where profitable goods should have been delivered. Ghostly outlines show what should be there (the expected profit). A ledger open showing the calculation: contract price minus market price equals damages. The merchant's expression is one of measured calculation, not anger.

## Chapter 25 — Defective Performance
A newly built cottage with visible defects — a crooked chimney, a leaking roof, mismatched windows. The homeowner and builder stand before it with competing estimates: one paper shows "Cost to Repair: 500 gold," another shows "Diminished Value: 200 gold." The cottage is livable but flawed.

## Chapter 26 — Limits on Damages
A river with three dams blocking the flow of gold coins (representing damages). First dam: "Foreseeability" (Hadley v. Baxendale). Second dam: "Certainty" (speculative profits blocked). Third dam: "Mitigation" (plaintiff must act). Some coins make it through all three, others are caught.

## Chapter 27 — Alternative Remedies
A judge's chamber with four doors, each marked with a different remedy: "Specific Performance" (a unique artifact behind the door), "Rescission" (hands giving back), "Restitution" (scales rebalancing), "Injunction" (a stop sign). The plaintiff stands choosing. The ordinary door marked "Money Damages" is already open behind them.

## Chapter 28 — Third-Party Beneficiaries
Three figures in a triangle arrangement. Two shake hands over a contract (the promisor and promisee). The third figure — standing apart but illuminated by a beam of light from the contract — is the intended beneficiary. In shadow behind the beneficiary, a fourth figure (incidental beneficiary) receives no light.
